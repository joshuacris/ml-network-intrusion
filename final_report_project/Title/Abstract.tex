\begin{center}
{\Large \textbf{ABSTRACT}}
\end{center}

\begin{spacing}{1.3}
\setlength{\parskip}{0.22in}

This project compares four supervised models for binary network intrusion
detection on the UNSW-NB15 dataset: Logistic Regression, Multi-Layer
Perceptron (MLP), Random Forest, and XGBoost. After removing 26{,}387
duplicate training rows, grouping rare categorical values, applying log
transformations to 14 highly skewed numerical features, and building
model-specific tabular representations with 58 predictors, we trained on
55{,}945 flows and evaluated on a held-out test set of 107{,}740 flows.
Hyperparameters were tuned with RandomizedSearchCV using 3-fold
cross-validation and ROC-AUC, and final classification thresholds were chosen
by sweeping thresholds from 0.01 to 0.99 to maximize attack-class F1. All
models improved substantially after threshold tuning: Logistic Regression
reached a tuned F1 of 0.9080, MLP 0.9121, Random Forest 0.9091, and XGBoost
0.9154. XGBoost also achieved the best validation ROC-AUC (0.9897) and test
ROC-AUC (0.9761), while Logistic Regression remained the most interpretable
baseline and the tree-based models achieved the strongest overall ranking
performance. Per-attack-category analysis showed that Analysis was the hardest
attack family across models, while Generic, Backdoor, Worms, and Exploits were
detected almost perfectly. Overall, the comparison shows that nonlinear
ensemble methods are best suited to this tabular intrusion-detection task, and
XGBoost provides the best overall trade-off between detection quality,
robustness, and practical usefulness.
\end{spacing}
